The Daily Loss Limit is calculated using the formula:

Start-of-day equity - the lowest floating equity of the day must not exceed the allowed percentage.

This rule applies to both open (floating) and closed trades.

Here are the Daily Loss Limits for each account type:

  1. 1. 1-Step: 4%
  2. 2. 2-Step: 4%
  3. 3. 3-Step: 5%
  4. 4. Instant: N/A

Example:

For a $100,000 Fewpips 1-Step Account:

The daily loss limit is 4%, which equals $4,000.

  • * If the trader starts the day at $100,000, the trader cannot lose more than $4,000 in total during that day.
  • * If the trader first makes a $2,000 profit, the daily limit increases to $6,000 for that day ($4,000 + $2,000 profit). If the total loss reaches that amount, including open trades, the account will be breached.
  • * If the account is $100,000 with a 4% Daily Loss Limit and a 10% Maximum Loss Limit, and the trader loses $4,000 in one day, they will breach the Daily Loss Limit making the trader breach the account. 
The Maximum Loss Limit (MLL) is a trailing loss limit that moves up as the account makes profits, but it never moves down after losses, and it can never go above the starting balance.

On the Fewpips Instant Account, the MLL is a trailing Maximum Loss Limit.

Key points:

  • * It only increases with profit.
  • * It never decreases with losses.
  • * It is capped at the initial account balance.
  • * Withdrawals do not reset or reduce the MLL; it stays at the highest level it has ever reached.

Example:

For a $10,000 Fewpips Instant Account (7% trailing Maximum Loss Limit).

Start: Equity $10,000 → Maximum Loss Limit breach point = $9,300 (7% below $10,000).

  • * After +$200 profit: Equity $10,200 → Maximum Loss Limit moves up to $9,500.
  • * Then -$100 loss: Equity $10,100 → Maximum Loss Limit stays at $9,500 (it never moves down).
  • * Then +$300 profit: Equity $10,400 → Maximum Loss Limit moves up to $9,700
  • * Then +$400 profit: Equity $10,700 → Maximum Loss Limit reaches $10,000 (capped at the initial balance).

If the equity is $10,500 and your MLL is $10,000, you have a $500 buffer. If you withdraw $300, your equity drops to $10,200, but the MLL stays at $10,000. The buffer just shrank from $500 to $200. If the equity ever touches or goes below the active Maximum Loss Limit, it is a breach.

Price movements are measured differently depending on the asset class and the level of precision required.

The "Point" (Full Unit)

A Point is a full unit change in price (the digit before the decimal). This is commonly used for Commodities (Gold, Oil) and Indices (US30, NAS100).

  • Example (US30): A move from 35,000 to 35,001 = 1 Point move.

The "Pip" (Standard Increment)

Short for "Percentage in Point", a Pip is the standard unit for Forex trading.

  • Most Pairs: 1 Pip = 4th decimal place (0.0001)
  • JPY Pairs: 1 Pip = 2nd decimal place (0.01)
  • Example (EURUSD): 1.1000 → 1.1001 = 1 Pip

The "Pipette" (Precision)

A Pipette is 1/10th of a Pip (5th decimal place). It allows ultra-precise pricing in liquid markets.

  • Example (EURUSD): 1.10001 → 1.10002 = 1 Pipette

How do you Calculate Profit & Loss (P&L)?

Your P&L depends on the price movement distance, lot size, and pip/point value.

Formula:
Profit/Loss = Pip or Point Difference × Lot Size × Pip Value

Example: Buying 1 Lot of XAUUSD (Gold)

  • Entry: 1892.00
  • Exit: 1892.70
  • Difference: 7 Pips (0.7 Points)
  • Calculation: 7 × 1.0 × $10.00 = +$70.00

Example: Selling 1 Lot of US30 (Dow Jones)

  • Entry: 34567.10
  • Exit: 34562.10
  • Difference: 5 Points
  • Calculation: 5 × 1.0 × $10.00 = +$50.00

Slippage: The Reality of Fast Markets

Slippage occurs when your order is executed at a different price than the one you requested.

  • Why it happens: During high volatility (news events) or low liquidity (market open), the requested price may not exist anymore. The system executes your order at the next available price.
  • Market Gaps: If the market jumps over the weekend, trades are filled at the new opening price. Fewpips does not adjust prices for natural market gaps.

Floating P&L: Unrealised Risk

Floating P&L represents the live profit or loss on trades that are still open.

  • It directly affects your Equity and Margin Level in real time.
  • Warning: Large floating losses can breach your Daily Loss Limit even if the trade is not closed.

Swap: Holding Costs

A Swap is the interest fee charged (or earned) when you hold a trade overnight past the daily server reset.

Leverage: Power and Responsibility

Leverage allows you to control a large trading position using a relatively small margin (security deposit).

Example: With 1:100 leverage, a $1.08 million EURUSD position (10 lots) requires only about $10,880 margin.

Danger of Overleveraging: Using more than 20–30% of total margin is considered high-risk. Overleveraged accounts are extremely sensitive to small price moves and often result in stop-outs or account breaches.

Margin (also called Margin Used) is a security deposit - not a fee - that the platform locks to cover potential losses on your active trades.

Example Calculation

If you open a 1.50 lot trade on AUDUSD using 1:30 leverage on a $5,000 account:

  • Total Value: 1.50 lots × 100,000 = $150,000
  • Position Value: 150,000 ÷ 30 (leverage) = $4,473
  • Required Margin: $4,473

Remaining Free Margin = $5,000 - $4,473 = $527

How to Calculate Margin Utilization

Margin Utilization measures how much of your account is being used to support open positions.

Formula:
Margin Utilization (%) = (Used Margin ÷ Total Account Balance) × 100

Example: ($3,149.10 ÷ $5,000) × 100 = 62.98%

Risk Levels

Margin Utilization Risk Level Trading Status
0% – 20% Low (Safe) Ideal flexibility and safe trading
21% – 50% Moderate Manageable but requires close monitoring
51% – 80% High Dangerous - risk of margin call
81% – 100% Extreme Immediate stop-out risk - cannot open trades

Best Practice

Fewpips recommends keeping Margin Utilization below 20% for long-term success and proper risk management.

  • Buffer Zone: If you have $5,000 and use only $1,000 margin, you have $4,000 free margin.
  • Opportunity Cost: High utilization limits your ability to enter new trades.
  • Compliance: Low utilization keeps you safe from margin calls and breaches.

Impact of Leverage

  • High Leverage (e.g. 1:100): Requires less margin but increases risk.
  • Low Leverage (e.g. 1:10): Requires more margin but improves risk control.
